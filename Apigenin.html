The benefits of flavonoids; in particular Apigenin

Plant derived extracts have been studied and used for their medicinal properties since the Ancient Egyptian period. In early Science, plant extracts were thought to cure a range of diseases with the addition of ritual practices such as ‘magic’. Throughout the centuries, Science has evolved where scientists now know that it is chemicals within plant extracts that have an effect on the organism, and not plants extracts alongside the superstition of magical rituals. This has come about from trial and error judgments on animals and humans subjects to the study of specific chemicals using high tech technologies, methods and statistical analysis backed by years of research today. (Parkins, 2001) Plant extracts contain chemicals such as vitamins, minerals and antioxidants to name a few. Phenolics are classed as antioxidants and are involved with providing protection for the plant, such as UV protection, antimicrobial, antiseptic, anti-cancerous and disease deflecting properties (Graham et al, 2003). Phenolics have derivatives including Tannins and Flavonoids, which have the same structural backbone consisting of one or two phenolic groups and are amongst the most widely, researched secondary metabolite due to their easy synthesis.

Flavonoids

First referred to as vitamin P and discovered by Hungarian physiologist Albert Szent-Györgyi in the mid 1930s. Flavonoids were thought to help metabolise vitamin C and that a vitamin P and C deficiency combined led to scurvy (Szent-Györgyi, 1938). Since then however the name vitamin P has gradually been replaced with the more acceptable flavonoids. This is probably due to the fact that according to The Oxford Dictionary a vitamin is:

‘…any group of compounds which are essential for normal growth and nutrition and are required in small quantities in the diet because they cannot be synthesised by the body.’ (Oxford University Press, 2012)

Flavonoids therefore cannot be referred to as a vitamin even though they may be beneficial in the diet, because simply put, they are not essential. Flavonoids are in fact plant pigments that are polyphenol compounds, these pigmentations help protect plants from insects and microorganisms.

They are found in fruits, vegetables, herbs, some grains and chocolate. Flavonoids have been used in traditional herbal remedies for thousands of years in eastern countries. Flavonoids have been found to have many beneficial effects on health such as anti-inflammatory, anti-viral and anti-bacterial properties (Hoorn et al. 2001) as well as preventing cell proliferation (Cook and Samman. 1996), anti-platelet aggregation and anti-thrombogenic effects (Middleton et al. 2000) amongst many others. Flavonoids are subdivided into groups of flavonols, flavonones, flavones, isoflavones and anthocyanidins depending on their chemical structure. Flavones are yellows pigments that can be found in cereals and herbs (Cermak & Wolffram,2006). Over 700 flavones have been identified (Renée and Williams, 2004) and of which Apigenin is an interesting substance.

Apigenin is a crystalline with molecular formula C15H10O5 and a melting point of 345-350˚c. First mentioned as a ‘gelatinous substance’ after cooling (Rump,1836), and later coined as ‘Apigenin’ when it was crystallised from parsley glycosides by Lindenborn who thought it was a chemical (Tipson and Wolfrom, 1968). The western diet consists of 20-50mg of flavonoids daily through a variety of foods eaten (Cermak & Wolffram,2006). Apigenin has been shown to act as an inhibitor on the protein cytochrome P4502C9 (Fawcett et al. 2009). Cytochrome P4502C9 is an enzyme found in the liver that metabolises many pharmaceutical drugs. It has also been to shown to reverse the effects of immunosuppressive drug Cyclosporine (Chong et al. 2009). In a more recent study by Mule et al (2009) the mechanism of action on gastric relaxation has found that gastric myorelaxant effects of Apigenin arose from negative modulation of an influx of calcium through voltage dependant Ca2+ channels.

Organisation of the gut

The gut also known as the gastrointestinal tract consists of a tube that is 10 metres in length running from the mouth to the anus. It consists of a series of organs that help digest food therefore having a problem in any one of these can result in serious diseases. The gut is organised starting from the mouth where food it chewed in small bits, enough to swallow down the oesophagus. Next comes the stomach where it breaks down even further turning it into chyme and killing off any microorganisms with concentrated hydrochloric acid. Once in chyme form, it enters the small intestine first where a variety of juices such as bile and pancreatic enzymes break it down further in simpler form of lipids, carbohydrates, proteins, vitamins, minerals, water and waste. The small intestine has villi covering its wall, thus increasing surface area, which allows maximum absorption of these nutrients. Next comes the large intestine, which absorbs remaining water and minerals and where bacteria break down waste material moving the waste towards the rectum. By now the waste material has reached the end of the gut tube, and the final sphincter of the anus muscle is voluntary whereby it is controlled by the organism of when it wants to open its bowels and empty the contents.

Smooth muscle

Smooth muscle is an involuntary muscle in that it cannot be stopped voluntarily; it is under the control of the autonomic nervous system. It is found in the walls of organs such as intestine, uterus, eye, urethra and blood vessels. Smooth muscle is made up of thick and think filaments (myosin and actin respectively) which are placed near the plasma membrane. The myosin and actin mediate by crosslinking thereby producing a contraction. This contraction is regulated by hormones and nerves. Contractions are initiated by an influx of calcium known as phosphorylation; this however differs to that of striated muscle in that calcium binds to myosin and not to troponin.

Gastrointestinal Disorders

Gastrointestinal disorders affect 1 in 5 people in the United Kingdom alone. The main causes of these disorders are infections from microorganisms, which if left to exacerbate can cause cancer of any gastric organ. General symptoms of gastric illness include pain, inflammation, nausea, vomiting, indigestion, heartburn and loss of appetite (NHS, 2012). An example of a popular microorganism that affects the intestines is the gram-negative bacteria Helicobacter.pylori (H.pylori). It affects 1 in 3 people every year (HPA, 2012). H.pylori is a commensal bacterium that is part of the body’s normal flora in the gut. It lives on the mucosal lining of the intestine. H.pylori is classed as an opportunistic pathogen in that it can affect the host if it gets passed the mucosal layer. If this happens, the immune system fires a response but cannot get past the stomach, as the concentrated gastric juices kill them off. However, the immune response does not give up and keeps sending killer cells. This overburden of immune cells causes the inflammation of the lining, which if left can lead to gastric ulcers (Helicobacter Foundation, 2006). As with all gram-negative bacteria, the outer membrane of H.pylori is made up of lipopolysaccharides which induces a strong immune response by their host. As the effects of Apigenin on microbial and anti-inflammatory effects have been observed, studying the vasorelaxant effects of on ileum could help sufferers of gastro related disorders, as Apigenin maybe a potential for a new therapeutic drug that could help ease the symptoms associated with gastric disorders.
